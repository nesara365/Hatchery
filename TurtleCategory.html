Green Turtle



The green turtle is one of the largest marine turtles and the only herbivore among the several types. The term "green turtle" relates to the turtles' fat and cartilage, not their shells, which have a greenish tint. Those who live in the Eastern Pacific call a group of green turtles with darker shells "black turtles." Green turtles are primarily found in tropical and subtropical waters. They move far between their birth beaches and where they feed, just like other sea turtles do. The overharvesting of green turtle eggs, the killing of adult turtles, getting caught in fishing gear, and the disappearance of breeding beaches are all dangers to the species, which is categorized as endangered.

Leatherback Turtle



They are among the largest sea turtle species and among the most migratory, crossing both the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans. Pacific leatherback turtles migrate to the California coast from their nesting grounds in the Coral Triangle every summer and fall to feed on the abundance of jellyfish. Despite being widely spread, leatherback turtle populations have severely declined during the past century as a result of excessive egg harvesting and bycatch in fisheries.

Hawksbill Turtle



The word "hawksbill" refers to a pointed beak. On their shells, they have a peculiar pattern of interlocking scales that give the edges a serrated appearance. Due to their unique colors and patterns, their shells are quite precious and frequently referred to as "tortoiseshell" in marketplaces. Hawksbills are primarily found in coral reefs in the tropical oceans of the world. They mostly eat sponges, which they pull from reef crevices using their tiny, pointed beaks, although they also consume sea anemones and jellyfish.

Loggerhead Turtle



Loggerhead turtles have huge heads that support powerful jaw muscles that allow them to crush hard-shelled prey like clams and sea urchins. They are less likely to be murdered for their meat or shell compared to other sea turtles. The unintended capture of marine species in fishing gear is known as bycatch, and because loggerhead turtles frequently interact with fisheries, it is a serious problem for them. The loggerhead turtle, which breeds on beaches from Greece and Turkey to Israel and Libya, is the most common species of turtle in the Mediterranean. Many of their nesting beaches are now in jeopardy due to the growth of the tourism sector.

Olive Ridley Turtle



The name of this sea turtle refers to the olive green color that may be seen on its shell. They are currently the most common sea turtles. They only nest in a few number of places, therefore any threat to one of the nest beaches could have a big effect on the entire population.
